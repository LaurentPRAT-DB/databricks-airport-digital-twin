# What-If Scenario Report: DXB Shamal Sandstorm

**Airport:** Dubai International Airport (OMDB/DXB)
**Scenario:** Shamal Sandstorm — Arabian Gulf
**Simulation Date:** 2026-03-15 (24-hour simulation)
**Total Scheduled Flights:** 1,030 (522 arrivals / 508 departures)
**Report Generated:** 2026-03-29

---

## Executive Summary

This simulation models a severe Shamal sandstorm impacting Dubai International Airport over a 24-hour period. The storm brings near-zero visibility (0.25 nm), sand accumulation on runways and taxiways, and gusts up to 50 knots. The result: **57.4% on-time performance**, **68 cancellations**, **7 diversions to Abu Dhabi (AUH)**, and a **fuel shortage** that compounds the recovery. While DXB's on-time rate holds better than the JFK winter storm, the **4-hour full runway closure** and diversions to AUH create significant passenger displacement.

**Key Finding:** The transition from "moderate dust" to "severe sandstorm" at 04:00 is abrupt and catches operations mid-flow with 164 simultaneous flights. Airports in the Gulf region must have pre-positioned sand sweeping equipment and formal diversion agreements with alternate airports (AUH, SHJ) to handle the sudden onset.

## Critical Events Timeline

### Phase 1: Dust Onset (02:00-04:00 UTC)

- **02:00** — Moderate dust reduces visibility to 1.5 nm. IFR conditions begin.
  - Airlines begin proactive cancellations: FDB2458, UAE2937, UAE1576, BAW2150, THY224 among the first 68 flights cancelled.
  - Departure holds begin: QFA1355 held at 02:04 due to departure rate at capacity.
- **02:04** — QFA2454 executes go-around on IFR approach. First indication of deteriorating conditions.

**Operator Action Required:** Activate sandstorm operations plan. Alert all ground crews for potential equipment protection. Begin discussions with AUH for diversion acceptance capacity.

### Phase 2: Severe Sandstorm — Full Closure (04:00-08:00 UTC)

This is the **critical 4-hour window** where DXB effectively shuts down:

- **04:00** — Severe sandstorm: 0.25 nm visibility, 200 ft ceiling, 50 kt gusts. LIFR.
  - **Runway 28L closed for 240 min** (4 hours) — sand accumulation requires sweeping.
  - Gate C24 fails — inaccessible for 5 hours (sand infiltration into jetbridge).
  - **Taxiway M closed** due to sand drift for **6 hours** — critical north-south ground route severed.
- **04:08** — Arrival holds escalate: UAE206, UAE1723, UAE2484 all holding.
- **04:52** — UAE206 go-around after extended hold. Fuel situation critical.
- **05:00** — **Runway 28R also closed for 180 min** (3 hours). **Airport fully closed.**
  - **7 diversions to Abu Dhabi declared:**
    - UAE206, UAE1723, UAE2484, CPA473 — all diverted at 05:00
    - UAE1767 — diverted at 05:57
    - Two additional diversions follow
  - Gate B15 fails at 04:00 — inaccessible for 4 hours. Two gates now OOS.

**Operator Action Required:** Declare airport closure. Coordinate with Abu Dhabi (AUH) and Sharjah (SHJ) for diversion capacity. Activate bus/coach transfers for diverted passengers. Deploy sand sweeping crews to priority runway. Protect ground equipment from sand damage.

### Phase 3: Gradual Recovery (08:00-14:00 UTC)

- **08:00** — Weather moderates to "moderate dust" (1.0 nm vis, 600 ft ceiling, 38 kt gusts). Runway 28R re-opens first.
  - Arrival rate severely reduced — single runway operation in IFR.
  - Departure backlog begins clearing.
- **10:00** — **Fuel shortage begins.** Fuel supply disrupted for 3 hours. Aircraft unable to refuel for departure, creating gate blocking.
  - This compounds the recovery: aircraft parked at gates cannot depart, preventing arriving flights from accessing gates.
- **11:00** — Haze clears to 4.0 nm visibility, MVFR conditions. Both runways operational.
- **13:00** — Fuel supply restored. Departure queue begins moving.

**Operator Action Required:** Prioritize fuel for long-haul aircraft with connection-critical passengers. Implement gate juggling — tow unfueled aircraft to remote stands. Sequence departures by fuel availability, not schedule order.

### Phase 4: Full Recovery (14:00-24:00 UTC)

- **14:00** — Clear conditions. VFR. All systems operational.
- Capacity events continue as the 387 total holds work through the system.
- Taxiway M reopens after 6-hour sand drift clearance.
- All gates restored to service.

## Risk Assessment

### High Risk — Full Airport Closure (05:00-08:00)

With both runways closed for 3+ hours, DXB has zero capacity. This is particularly dangerous for DXB because:
- It handles significant long-haul traffic (Emirates hub) — these aircraft cannot easily divert to distant alternates
- Peak Gulf region traffic coincides with early morning (02:00-06:00 UTC), exactly when the storm hits
- AUH diversion capacity has limits — 7 diversions absorbed, but a larger storm could overwhelm AUH

**Mitigation:** Establish formal LOA with AUH and SHJ for guaranteed diversion slots during sandstorm events. Pre-position sand sweeping equipment to enable faster runway clearing. Consider closing 28L proactively at 03:00 to begin sweeping before peak accumulation.

### High Risk — Fuel Shortage During Recovery

The fuel shortage at 10:00-13:00 hits exactly when operations are trying to recover. Aircraft blocking gates because they can't refuel creates a vicious cycle:
- Gate blocked → arriving flight holds on taxiway → taxiway congestion → departures delayed
- This cascade turned a 4-hour weather event into a 10-hour operational disruption

**Mitigation:** Maintain 48-hour fuel reserve at DXB tank farm. Establish emergency fuel supply agreement with ADNOC (Abu Dhabi). Pre-position fuel bowsers during sandstorm warnings for immediate deployment post-storm.

### Medium Risk — Taxiway Sand Drift

Taxiway M closure for 6 hours forces all north-south ground traffic onto alternate routes, increasing taxi times and creating bottlenecks. Sand drift is a unique Gulf-region risk that requires specialized sweeping equipment.

**Mitigation:** Install sand fences along vulnerable taxiway segments. Pre-position mobile sweepers at Taxiway M intersections. Develop alternate taxi routing plans specifically for Taxiway M closure scenarios.

### Medium Risk — Gate Jetbridge Sand Infiltration

Two gate failures (C24 for 5h, B15 for 4h) due to sand infiltration demonstrate that ground infrastructure is vulnerable to sandstorm damage. With 20 gates at full utilization, losing 10% of gate capacity compounds all other delays.

**Mitigation:** Install sand seals on jetbridge mechanisms. Establish rapid-response maintenance team for gate equipment during sandstorm events. Pre-assign remote stand positions for use when gates are OOS.

## Recommendations for Airport Preparedness

1. **Pre-storm runway clearing rotation:** When sandstorm forecast confirms >2h of severe conditions, begin proactive sand sweeping on one runway while the other handles reduced traffic. Never allow simultaneous dual closure.

2. **Formal diversion LOA with AUH/SHJ:** Establish minimum 15-slot diversion capacity guarantee with AUH. Pre-position Emirates ground handling staff at AUH during sandstorm season (March-July).

3. **Fuel supply resilience:** The 3-hour fuel shortage during recovery was the single biggest amplifier of delays. Maintain on-site 72-hour fuel reserve. Establish pipeline bypass procedure for emergency resupply.

4. **Sand-hardened infrastructure:** Invest in sand seals for jetbridges, covered taxiway segments for critical routes, and permanent sand fences along Taxiway M.

5. **Communication with long-haul operators:** Emirates and other hub carriers need 6-hour advance notice of potential closure to adjust inbound traffic. Holding A380s for 4 hours is not sustainable — early diversions save fuel and reduce risk.

6. **Ground crew protection:** Sandstorm conditions (50 kt gusts, low visibility) are hazardous for ramp workers. Ensure all ground operations cease during severe phase and resume only with proper PPE.

---

## Conclusion

The DXB Shamal simulation demonstrates that Gulf-region airports face unique challenges from sand-related disruptions that differ significantly from rain or snow events. The **fuel shortage during recovery** is the most actionable finding — it's preventable with proper supply chain planning and turns a 4-hour weather event into a 10-hour disruption. The 7 diversions to AUH underscore the need for formal mutual-aid agreements between Gulf airports. DXB's higher base on-time rate (57.4% vs JFK's 23%) reflects better infrastructure for its typical weather, but the airport remains vulnerable to the abrupt onset of Shamal conditions.
